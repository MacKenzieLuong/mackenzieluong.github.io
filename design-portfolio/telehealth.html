Project Overview

In partnership with a Nursing student, I designed UI improvements for Telerehabilitation Collaboration, a platform connecting clinicians across healthcare organizations to share expertise and resources. This was my first experience working with real stakeholders through bi-weekly progress meetings.

My Role: UI/UX Designer, responsible for visual design, information architecture, and stakeholder communication

Tools: Figma, stakeholder interviews, healthcare accessibility guidelines (WCAG 2.1 AA)

Timeline: October 2024 - January 2025 with bi-weekly stakeholder check-ins

Note: Due to confidentiality agreements, I cannot share full designs but can discuss process, methodology, and learnings.

The Problem

The Telerehabilitation Collaboration platform needed UI improvements to better serve clinicians managing patient care across multiple healthcare organizations. The primary challenges were:

  • Legacy System Constraints: Healthcare applications typically run on older systems - the goal wasn't modernization, but clarity within existing technical limitations
  • Multi-Organization Scaling: Design needed to accommodate future partner organizations without looking fragmented or disconnected
  • Consistency Requirements: Maintaining visual and interaction consistency so additional partner applications would feel cohesive, not like separate extensions
  • Clinical Workflow Integration: Interface had to fit into busy clinicians' existing workflows without adding cognitive load

Research & Discovery

Competitive Analysis: Analyzed existing telehealth platforms (Doxy.me, SimplePractice, TheraNest) to understand common patterns and identify gaps in rehabilitation-specific features.

Healthcare Standards Research: Studied WCAG 2.1 guidelines and HIPAA requirements to ensure compliance-first design approach.

User Interviews: My nursing partner facilitated interviews with 3 clinicians and helped me understand patient needs through her clinical experience.

üí° Key Insights

  • Forgiveness in Design: Elderly patients often abandon apps after single failure - error prevention is critical
  • Efficiency First: Clinicians need quick patient overview, not click-heavy detailed views
  • Reliability > Quality: Video quality matters less than connection reliability for remote areas
  • Trust Building: Patients need constant reassurance about data privacy

Design Process

01

User Research (Weeks 1-2)

Worked with nursing partner to understand clinical workflows. Reviewed existing telehealth platforms. Identified accessibility requirements through WCAG research.

02

Information Architecture (Week 3)

Created user flows for key tasks: appointment booking, video consultations, exercise tracking. Prioritized simplicity - no task should require more than 3 taps.

03

Low-Fi Wireframes (Week 4-5)

Sketched wireframes focusing on large touch targets (minimum 44x44px), clear labels, and logical flow. Got feedback from nursing partner on clinical accuracy.

04

High-Fidelity Mockups (Week 6-10)

Built comprehensive Figma prototype with color choices meeting 4.5:1 contrast ratios, system fonts for reliability, and clear visual states for all interactive elements.

Key Design Decisions

üì± Progressive Disclosure

Decision: Show only essential info by default, hide advanced features
Rationale: Reduces cognitive load for elderly users while keeping power features accessible to clinicians.

üé® Color as Secondary Indicator

Decision: Never rely solely on color to convey information
Rationale: Accommodates color-blind users (8% of men). Added icons and text labels to all color-coded elements.

üìù Plain Language

Decision: Avoid medical jargon in patient-facing interfaces
Rationale: "Check-in" instead of "Patient Assessment Portal." Clarity trumps clinical precision for patients.

üîî Proactive Guidance

Decision: Show next steps and helpful hints before users need them
Rationale: Prevents abandonment by reducing moments of confusion, especially for first-time users.

üîê Visible Privacy Controls

Decision: Make security features prominent, not hidden in settings
Rationale: Builds trust - patients need constant reassurance their health data is protected.

‚ö° Offline-First Approach

Decision: Design for graceful degradation with poor connectivity
Rationale: Rural users may have spotty internet. Show what's accessible offline and clear sync indicators.

Reflection & Personal Growth

‚úì What This Project Taught Me

  • Domain expertise matters: I couldn't design effectively without understanding clinical workflows. Collaborating with my nursing partner was essential.
  • Accessibility is not optional: Designing for my grandparents' generation made accessibility personal. I learned it's not just about compliance - it's about respect.
  • Constraints drive better design: WCAG requirements and HIPAA limitations forced me to think creatively about solutions.
  • Healthcare is high-stakes: Unlike my game design work, mistakes here could impact patient care. This raised my standards for clarity and error prevention.

‚ñ≥ What I'd Do Differently

  • Usability testing: Academic constraints limited real user testing - would prioritize this in future healthcare projects
  • Technical feasibility check: Some designs may have been challenging to implement - would loop in developers earlier
